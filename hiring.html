Hiring programmers

Stories often come up on HN about hiring in tech, like:

  • https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=16874015
  • https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=16877320
  • etc.
A few common themes have emerged:
  • It's hard to filter good applicants so companies make the process harder...
  • ... the process gets harder so applicants complain.
      • Let's discuss both sides of the argument.

      Hiring

      There are companies that have dozens or hundreds of good programmers applying for jobs day in, day out. Most companies are not like that. My experience has been that, in a big company in an attractive market, out of 50 applicants:
      • 10 had little to no relevant skills (eg. support instead of dev)
      • 5 had exactly what we wanted
      • In between, we had lots of candidate with relevant experience, but surprisingly little to show for it[1]
      Now, hiring the 5 we liked wasn't always possible. They'd get offers from competitors, and sometimes take them. In the end, it tooks months of sitting through interviews going nowhere to end up hiring one candidate. And then... there's still the possibility of a bad hire. Someone who looked good to whoever interviewed him, but turned out to not be quite as good as he or she appeared. After having this happen to your team once or twice, it's understandable that you'd want a stricter hiring process:
      1. Filter out bad candidates earlier on to reduce the number of useless interviews
      2. Have a better idea of what candidates can do to reduce the number of bad hires
      [1]: a word about people who spend 10 years writing bad code and not understanding the business they work for.
